The Problem

African healthcare
runs on paper
and memory

Walk into any private clinic in Wandegeya. The patient register is an exercise book. Prescriptions are written by hand and carried to a pharmacy that has no idea they're coming. Lab results come back on paper, if they come back at all.

This isn't a technology problem. It's an infrastructure problem. The connected layer that makes modern healthcare possible — patient records that travel, facilities that see each other's work, data that informs decisions — was never built for Africa.

Minza is building it from scratch. Starting with the 3-room clinic in Wandegeya. Scaling to every facility on the continent.

📋
No patient history
Records live in exercise books. Lost books mean lost histories. No continuity across visits or facilities.
💊
Prescriptions lost in transit
Doctor writes, patient carries paper to pharmacy. No tracking, no confirmation, no record.
🏥
Facilities in silos
Clinics, labs, and pharmacies cannot see each other's work. Every handoff is manual and invisible.
📊
No data for anyone
Governments can't see epidemics. Insurers can't verify claims. Facilities can't manage stock smartly.

About

Built in
Kampala.
For Africa.

Minza started with a simple observation: every private clinic in Wandegeya was running on an exercise book. Not because the staff weren't capable — but because no one had built the right software for them.

The infrastructure that Epic, Waystar, and Health Catalyst built for developed markets was never built for Africa. We're building it from scratch — designed for the 3-room clinic, scalable to the national hospital network.

The operations software gets you installed. The network effect — patient records moving between facilities, drug procurement intelligence, population health data for governments — is what makes Minza impossible to replace. The clinic in Wandegeya is where it starts. The continent is where it ends.

Founder & CEO, Minza Health · Building in Kampala, Uganda
