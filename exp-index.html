The Problem

African healthcare runs on paper and memory

A patient walks into a clinic in Wandegeya. Their entire medical history is on a worn exercise book — if they remembered to bring it. The doctor writes a prescription by hand. The pharmacy has no idea it's coming. The lab result goes back on paper.

This isn't a technology problem. It's an infrastructure problem. The connected layer that makes modern healthcare possible was never built for Africa.

Minza builds it.

📋
No patient history continuity Records live in paper exercise books. Lost books mean lost histories. Facilities have no visibility into a patient's prior care.
💊
Prescriptions die in transit Doctor writes, patient carries paper to pharmacy. No tracking. No confirmation. No record of what was dispensed.
🏥
Facilities operate in silos Clinics, labs, pharmacies, and hospitals cannot see each other's work. Every handoff is manual, prone to error, and invisible.
📊
No data for anyone Governments can't see epidemics forming. Insurers can't verify claims. Facilities can't manage stock intelligently.

About

Built in Kampala.

Minza started with a simple observation: every private clinic in Wandegeya was running on an exercise book. Not because the staff weren't capable — but because no one had built the right software for them.
