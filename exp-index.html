The Problem

Healthcare isn't broken
because of bad doctors.
It's broken underneath.

The systems that should connect clinics, labs, pharmacies, and governments simply don't exist. Every actor operates in isolation, and the patient pays for it at every handoff.

🗂️

Records live in your head

A patient walks into a new clinic has start from scratch. Previous diagnoses, allergies, chronic conditions all gone. The doctor has nothing so they guess, over-test, or miss something that someone else already caught.

~90% of records are still paper-based
🔌

Nothing talks to anything else

A prescription written at a clinic can't be verified at the pharmacy down the same street. A lab result has no way of reaching the referring doctor. An insurer gets a handwritten claim form. Every single handoff is literally a broken link.

Zero continent-wide data standard
📊

Governments are flying blind

Disease surveillance across most of Africa runs weeks or months behind. Outbreak detection, drug stock-outs, regional health trends are alll mostly estimates. The data exists in fragments because nobody has assembled it into something governments can actually use.

Policy made without real-time data

Why Now

Three things just became
simultaneously true.

Infrastructure businesses need specific conditions to work. We think those conditions have arrived, and they won't stay open indefinitely.

$259B

The African healthcare market is estimated to hit $259 billion by 2030 and almost none of the foundational data infrastructure has been built yet. The floor is completely open.

UHC

Uganda, Kenya, Rwanda, Nigeria and many other governments across the continent are pushing Universal Health Coverage. Every UHC programme needs exactly what we're building: an interoperable layer to manage claims, coverage, and outcomes.

4G+

Mobile penetration and 4G have hit critical mass across East Africa. A software infrastructure layer that needs reliable connectivity is finally deployable outside major cities. The window is now.

Our Why

The ordinary patient in Uganda has to carry a bunch of medical documents, often worn, to every appointment because those few papers hold their entire medical history. Pharmacists often dispense the wrong drug because the handwriting on the prescription slip is illegible. Insurance claims usually sit for months unattended because no one has a way of verifying anything.

These aren't edge cases, they're a normality in Uganda and Africa at large, and none of them are the fault of the doctors, pharmacists or even the patients involved. They're the fault of the missing infrastructure.

The countries that fixed this, Denmark, Singapore and many others did not do it by building better hospital management apps. They instead built the layer underneath first. Africa hasn't built that layer yet. We at Medicol, are going to.
